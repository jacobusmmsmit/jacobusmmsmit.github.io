  • When I moved to France for my year out to study, I was often asked how I learned French (and so quickly). My answer to these questions generally went:
    • I happened to listen to a lot of French music and heard how it sounds and picked up pronunciation and prosody.
    • I gradually accumulated a small vocabulary from repeated exposure.
    • I learned some phrases that were useful to me with the French speakers I knew.
    • I sat down one day and learned the verb tenses and grammar that is useful to me and easy to understand.
  • By the time I would get to the last step, my French accent was more or less better than most English people, and, while I couldn't form creative or correct sentences, I did know how to talk about things that mattered to me.
  • I was, of course, hugely benefitting from my already fluent Spanish. Hence, much of my vocabulary and grammar was merely francophised from either English or Spanish whenever I couldn't remember a word.
  • I also happened to take a module in French that lasted for the whole academic year. While the module may not have been so dense in its teachings, it did add some structure to my learning and made sure I understood the most basic grammar. Importantly, it also gave me some time to speak French, which was supplemented by my very kind friend Sixtine offering to speak to me for an hour each week in French. She was such an amazing asset to my learning as we were good friends and she did not pull any punches in point out my mistakes.
  • It is now 2 years since the day I moved to France. My French improved hugely thanks to the ~8 months I was there, but I recognise that this is not always an option for people. So now that I plan to learn Swedish, I am going to document all of the methods that I use to do it as quickly and effectively as possible.
    • Grammar Books
      • There are generally two levels of grammar books: those titled Essential and those titled Comprehensive. While I do find comprehensive grammars to be curious while learning about a language and its features and quirks, I do not find them to be good learning materials when starting out.
      • Essential grammars, on the other hand, do provide a great source of all of the basic aspects of a languages grammar, pronunciation, and some tips on modern usage. The one I am currently studying from is Swedish. An Essential Grammar - Holmes and Hinchliffe and it does a good job explaining things with a balance of linguistic rigour and kindness to learners.
      • This is not, however, the first Swedish book I used to learn the grammar. In fact, this was the only book about Swedish available in the University of Warwick e-library: The Acquisition of Swedish Grammar. This book focused on contributing to the theories of language acquisition, but also included an intense, linguistics jargon heavy introduction to the salient aspects of Swedish grammar which I found myself rapidly skimming over. I felt like it gave me a good overview of what to expect: a language that, much like English, is easy to communicate in but difficult to become native-level fluent in.
    • Children's Stories
      • Due to my upbringing in an Afrikaans speaking household, I can understand germanic languages better than most English people who have never actually learned germanic language (although this does depend on your definition of learn). This lends me to quickly trying to see the language in context in its simplest form which tends to be children's stories. That is not to say that these are at all a walk in the park â€” it is actually quite the opposite. As children learn to speak first and read later, books for children assume that they already have a grasp of the spoken language. This means that any number of difficult concepts can be thrown at you from the beginning, which can be quite frustrating.
      • My advice would be to try to find books that are mostly for adults to read to younger children. You know the sort: Max the Dog Buys Flowers for His Mum. Few words, simple sentences.
